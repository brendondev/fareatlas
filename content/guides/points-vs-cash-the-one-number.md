Every award booking comes down to one question: are your points buying you more than a cent each, or less? That number is **cents per point**, and it decides everything.

## The formula

Take the cash price of the exact same flight, subtract the taxes and fees you'd pay on the award, and divide by the number of points.

> **cents per point = (cash fare − award taxes) ÷ points × 100**

A Sydney–Tokyo Business seat sells for **$4,200**. The award costs **90,000 points + $180** in taxes. So:

- (4200 − 180) ÷ 90000 × 100 = **4.47 cents per point**

## What the number means

Australian frequent-flyer points are worth roughly **1.5 to 2 cents** each as a rule of thumb. So 4.47 cents is a strong redemption — you're getting more than double baseline value, and you should book it with points.

Flip it around. That same 90,000 points against a **$700** Economy fare with $80 taxes:

- (700 − 80) ÷ 90000 × 100 = **0.69 cents per point**

That's a poor redemption. Pay the $700, keep the points for the Business seat.

## Why this is the whole game

Points aren't money and they aren't free. They have a floating value that depends entirely on what you spend them against. The same 90,000 points were an excellent deal and a bad one in the two examples above — nothing about the points changed, only the cash fare they were measured against.

This is why FareAtlas shows you the cash fare next to the award every time. The points price alone tells you nothing.

## The one habit worth building

Before you burn points on anything, find the cash price of the identical flight and run the division. If it clears **2 cents**, book with points. If it's under **1 cent**, pay cash. Between the two, it's a judgement call about how much you value the cabin and how easily you earn more points.
